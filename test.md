#### 1. b)
Starting from the voltage divider equation, our goal is to write $\large R_{1}$ based on the other variables. $\large U_{out}=\dfrac{U_{in}\cdot R_{2}}{R_{1}+R_{2}}\Rightarrow R_{1}+R_{2}=\dfrac{U_{in}\cdot R_{2}}{U_{out}}$
Thus, $\large R_{1}=\dfrac{U_{in}\cdot R_{2}}{U_{out}}-R_{2}=\dfrac{R_{2}(U_{in}-U_{out})}{U_{out}}$. We can now do numerical substitution, taking into account that $\large U_{in}=9.6\ V$ and $\large U_{out}=5\ V$ $\large \Rightarrow R_{1}=\dfrac{50000\cdot 4.6}{5}=46000\ \ohm$. Thus, if $\large R_{2}=50\ k\ohm$, we should choose $\large R_{1}= 46\ k\ohm$ to safely measure the battery voltage.

Why is it a good idea to use large resistor values?
1. Power Dissipation: It is known that the current is inversely proportional to the resistance value. Using the $\large P=I^2R$ formula, the current can now be corelated to the heat generated by the resistors. Thus, large resistor values $\large \Rightarrow$ lower power dissipation and heat reduction.  
2. Signal integrity: If $\large R_{1}$ and $\large R_{2}$ have small values, they might draw significant current from the signal source (in our case, the battery), potentially affecting our measurements.
3. Component sizing: Even though this might seem insignificant, every gram matters when it comes to rocketry. By using large resistor values, as explained above, both the current value and the dissipated power will be lower, thus reducing the overall power rating needed for the resistors. Practically, that means a smaller footprint and also lower weight.

#### 1.g) 
If we're measuring voltages between $\large 0-5V$ using $\large 8$ bits, then $\large 0V$ will be equivalent to $\large 0000\,0000$ and $\large 5V$ to $\large 1111\,1111$.  In the same manner, using $\large 8$ bits, we can express decimal values ranging from $\large 2^0-1=0$ to $\large 2^8-1=255$. 

Linear interpolation offers a straightforward method for estimating intermediate voltages between known data points ($\large 0V$ and $\large 5V$ in this case). Using it, we can correlate the bit value $\large 137$ to a voltage. Let's say that our first pair of values is $\large (x_{1},y_{1})=(0,0)$. Accordingly, $\large (x_{2},y_{2})=(255,5)$. We want to find $\large y$ from the $\large (x,y)=(137,y)$ pair. Using the formula $\large y=y_{1}+\dfrac{y_{2}-y_{1}}{x_{2}-x_{1}}\cdot (x-x_{1})$ and plugging in the numbers, $\large y=0+\dfrac{5-0}{255-0}\cdot (137-0)\approx 2.686$. Thus, we are measuring a voltage of $\large \approx 2.686V$

An even easier way to get this result would be using the famous "Rule of Thirds". By leveraging the proportional relationship between the bit value and voltage range, this rule enables quick estimation of intermediate voltage values. We know that $\large 255$ corresponds to $\large 5V$, and $\large 137$ to a value that will be $\large xV$. According to the rule, $\large x=\dfrac{137\times 5}{255}\approx 2.686V$. As can be seen, we end up solving the same equation as above.

In order to determine the bit string corresponding to the decimal value $\large 137$, we use binary conversion. Starting with our given decimal value, we find its binary representation by successively dividing the value by 2 and noting the remainders. The remainder at each step corresponds to the binary digit ($\large 0$ or $\large 1$) at that position. Finally, reversing the sequence of remainders gives us the binary representation of the decimal value. 

Let's leave the theory aside and focus on our task:
- $\large 137/2=68r 1$
- $\large 68/2=34r 0$
- $\large 34/2=17r0$
- $\large 17/2=8r1$
- $\large 8/2=4r0$
- $\large 4/2=2r0$
- $\large 2/2=1r0$
- $\large 1/2=0r1$

Reading the remainders in reverse order gives us the binary representation $\large 1000\,1001$. Therefore, the decimal value $\large 137$ corresponds to the bit string $\large 1000\,1001$.

When it comes to resolution, knowing that $\large U_{out}$ is recorded as an $\large 8$-bit value, it can take decimal values from $\large 0$ to $\large 2^8-1=255$. Also, the full range of possible output voltages is between $\large 0V$ and $\large 5V$. Dividing the ceiling voltage to the number of decimal values ($\large 2^8$) will give us the voltage step size (or resolution). Thus, the resolution is $\Delta U_{out}=\large \dfrac{5}{256}\approx 0.0195$. 

To give a proper answer, we need to find the smallest temperature change that we can measure ($\large \Delta T$).
We know that our op-amp's gain is $\large G=12.5$. That means that for every step at the output of our circuit, we get an input $\large 12.5$ times smaller. Thus, $\large \Delta U_{LM35}=\dfrac{\Delta U_{out}}{G}=\dfrac{0.0195V}{12.5}\approx1.562mV$. This is our voltage step size seen from the $\large LM35$'s perspective. 

Finally, we need to correlate this to $\large \Delta T$. How? Linear interpolation once again!
Let's say that our first pair of values is $\large (x_{1},y_{1})=(0,0)$. Accordingly, $\large (x_{2},y_{2})=(0.4,40)$. We want to find $\large y$ from the $\large (x,y)=(1.562\cdot10^{-3},y)$ pair. Using the formula $\large y=y_{1}+\dfrac{y_{2}-y_{1}}{x_{2}-x_{1}}\cdot (x-x_{1})$ and plugging in the numbers, $\large y=0+\dfrac{40-0}{0.4-0}\cdot(1.562\cdot 10^{-3}-0)\approx 0.156$. There we have it - the smallest temperature change we can measure is $\large \Delta T\approx0.156\,^\circ C$.

As an alternative, we could've calculated $\large \Delta T$ using the output equation of $\large U_{LM35}$ but with a catch. Instead of the normal values $\large U_{LM35}$ and $\large T$, we will now use $\large \Delta U_{LM35}$ and $\large \Delta T$.
$\large \Delta U_{LM35}=\dfrac{\Delta T}{100\,^\circ C}\cdot V\Rightarrow$ $\large 1.562\cdot10^{-3}=\dfrac{\Delta T}{100}$ (now working unitless), thus $\large \Delta T=1.562\cdot10^{-1}\,^\circ C\approx0.156\,^\circ C$. The same answer, but with a simplified thought process.
